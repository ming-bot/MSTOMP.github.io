Stochastic Trajectory Optimization for Robotic Skill Acquisition From a Suboptimal Demonstration

Chenlin Ming, Zitong Wang, Boxuan Zhang, Zhanxiang Cao, Xiaoming Duan, Jianping He
IWIN-FINS Lab, Shanghai Jiao Tong University
Code arXiv

Abstract

Learning from Demonstration (LfD) has emerged as a crucial method for robots to acquire new skills. However, when given suboptimal task trajectory demonstrations with shape characteristics reflecting human preferences but subpar dynamic attributes such as slow motion, robots not only need to mimic the behaviors but also optimize the dynamic performance. In this work, we leverage optimization-based methods to search for a superior-performing trajectory whose shape is similar to that of the demonstrated trajectory. Specifically, we use Dynamic Time Warping (DTW) to quantify the difference between two trajectories and combine it with additional performance metrics, such as collision cost, to construct the cost function. Moreover, we develop a multi-policy version of the Stochastic Trajectory Optimization for Motion Planning (STOMP), called MSTOMP, which is more stable and robust to parameter changes. To deal with the jitter in the demonstrated trajectory, we further utilize the gain-controlling method in the frequency domain to denoise the demonstration and propose a computationally more efficient metric, called Mean Square Error in the Spectrum (MSES), that measures the trajectories' differences in the frequency domain. % 引入到频域 We also theoretically highlight the connections between the time domain and the frequency domain methods. Finally, we verify our method in both simulation experiments and real-world experiments, showcasing its improved optimization performance and stability compared to existing methods.

To generate demonstration trajectories, we utilize the TEACH and TEACHREPEAT functions of the Unitree Z1 Pro, which allow the robot to record human-guided movements. However, these demonstrations, while informative, are often inefficient, with suboptimal motion and noise that can hinder the robot's learning process. To address this, we apply our MSTOMP algorithm, which incorporates cost terms for smoothness and similarity to the original demonstration. This optimization process refines the robot’s motion, resulting in trajectories that are not only faster but also smoother than the original demonstrations. These experiments highlight MSTOMP's adaptability to different tasks, successfully preserving shape characteristics while optimizing dynamic performance.

To validate the effectiveness of our proposed MSTOMP framework, we conducted a series of experiments in both simulation and real-world scenarios. First, we compare MSTOMP to STOMP in simulation. Thanks to its multi-policy structure, MSTOMP converges faster, with smaller mean errors and lower variance, demonstrating improved stability and better exploration during optimization.
